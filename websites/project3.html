NOVA - Animal Shelter

case study

NOVA - Animal Shelter is a website and application project for a shelter for homeless dogs and cats in my hometown.

Project duration: March - April 2022

My role: UX researcher, UX designer

Responsibilities: user research, wireframing, prototyping, testing

Project overview

The problem:

  • I noticed that our shelter has huge communication problems, some of the community would like to help but are unable to because they do not even know that such help is needed.

The goal:

  • My goal is to improve communication between the shelter and the community willing to help.

User research: summary

  • I conducted interviews and created empathy maps to understand the users I’m designing for and their needs.
  • Overall, I distinguished two groups of people. The first group are people willing to help directly at the shelter, as volunteers. So far, these people have had problems with contact, they could not communicate with the shelter about the current needs, vacancies and schedules.
  • The second group of users are people who would simply like to help the shelter in other ways, adoption, but also material or financial help would be at stake. These people also have not been able to find the necessary information about what is needed in our shelter so far.

Starting the design

Ideation - Paper wireframes: Taking the time to iterate each application screen on paper has ensured that the items that ended up in the digital wireframes were well suited to solving user problems. From the very beginning, I wanted the application had utility functions and was extremely easy to use for people of all ages and levels of advancement.

Digital wireframes: When creating a digital mock-up, I tried to arrange the elements in such a way that it would always be easy to find a place where you can find contact information.

  • A. Contact details always available
  • B. The cta button with a reference to the contact visible on the main page project.

Low fidelity prototype:

When creating a digital wireframes I tried to arrange the elements in such a way that the screen was not oversaturated with information and the information easily and smoothly passed from screen to screen.

Usability study: parameters

  • 1

    Study type:

    Moderated usability study

  • 2

    Location:

    07.04 .2022, Katowice, Main Square 4

  • 3

    Participants:

    5 participants Two males, two females, and one nonbinary individual between the ages of 20 and 75. Two people from a larger city, three people from smaller towns, all participants are from the region of Silesia One participant is a person with a visual impairment. One user of assistive technologies (keyboard, screen reader)

  • 4

    Length:

    Each session will last about 15 minutes, and will include an introduction, a list of tasks, and a short questionnaire.

Usability study: findings

I conducted two rounds of usability studies. Findings from the first study helped guide the designs from wireframes to mockups. The second study used a low-fidelity prototype and revealed what aspects of the mockups needed refining.

  • 1

    Add financial info

    There was a big problem with finding information on financial aid, so the information architecture in this area needs to be rebuilt.

  • 2

    Menu - correct

    Most of the participants reported minor inaccuracies in navigation, the navigation needs to be improved and more readable.

  • 3

    Confirmation

    Some participants noticed the lack of confirmation in some activities, it will be necessary to add new information confirming the user's activities on the page.

Refining the design

Mockups: I improved the navigation to make the links more clearly expressing where they lead and I added confirmations to user actions.

High-fidelity prototype: The final high fidelity prototype represented a cleaner and more intuitive user workflow. It also satisfied the needs of users to add information about financial aid, a clearer navigation menu, and confirm user actions on the site.

Going forward

Takeaways

Impact:

The aim of my project was to provide a place that would be a source of reliable, readable and easily accessible information on current affairs in the shelter and to improve communication between the shelter and volunteers and other people who would like to support the shelter with any help. I think this goal has been achieved. The reviews from usability test participants are very optimistic.

What I learned:

Once again I found out that usability tests, even the shortest ones, always bring a positive value to the project. Empathy, reasearch and usability research are the most important processes in obtaining satisfactory results.
